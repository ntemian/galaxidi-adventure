1.2 Environmental Storytelling +

The most immersive games tell stories through the environment itself, not just dialog. Every object in a scene should tell you something about who lives there, what happened, or what's about to happen.

Principle â€” The Speaking Room

  2. What happened recently (an open book, a cold cup of coffee, moved furniture)
  3. What might happen next (a locked door, a half-packed suitcase, a letter on the table)

LucasArts mastered this. In Monkey Island 2, every room in the Voodoo Lady's shop told you about her character before she spoke a word. In Fate of Atlantis, the dusty shelves and broken pottery in the opening told you Sophia was passionate but chaotic.

1.3 The "First 30 Seconds" Rule +

When a player enters a new scene, they make an unconscious judgment in the first 30 seconds: "Is this world alive?"

What they notice (in order):

  1. Movement â€” Is anything moving? Particles, water, wind, light shifts
  2. Sound â€” Is there ambient sound that matches what they see?
  3. Response â€” Does the world respond to them? (hover highlights, entry dialog)
  4. Density â€” Are there enough objects to explore? (4-6 minimum per scene)

3.1 The Three Laws of Good Puzzles +
Law 1 â€” Fairness (The Ron Gilbert Rule)

The player must have ALL the information needed to solve a puzzle BEFORE they encounter it. No hidden rooms, no unexaminable objects, no knowledge that only exists in the designer's head. Ron Gilbert wrote this in his 1989 manifesto "Why Adventure Games Suck" â€” and it's still the golden rule.

Law 2 â€” Logic (The Chain Rule)

Every solution must follow from observable cause and effect. "Use key on lock" is logical. "Use monkey on pump" is not (yes, Monkey Island did this â€” even LucasArts broke their own rules). The test: could a smart 12-year-old figure this out?

Law 3 â€” Reward (The Dopamine Rule)

Solving a puzzle must feel better than the frustration of being stuck. This means: immediate feedback (new area, story progress, character reaction), not just a tiny inventory change.

6.1 The Iceberg Principle +

Hemingway's iceberg theory applies perfectly to game worlds: only show 10% of what you know. The other 90% gives the 10% its weight.

If you know that Chrysostomos served on twelve ships before the Elpida, you don't need to tell the player all twelve. But when he mentions "the winter of '82, when the waves took the Pandora," the player feels the depth of a life lived. One iceberg tip suggests the whole mountain underneath.

Principle â€” Let Players Discover, Not Be Told

Information found by the player is worth 10x information told to them. A letter discovered in a drawer hits harder than an NPC monologue delivering the same facts.

6.2 Consistency is Magic +

Magic doesn't feel like magic if the rules change. Fantasy worlds work because they have consistent internal logic. Your game world works the same way.

8.2 The Zeigarnik Effect â€” Unfinished Business +

In 1927, psychologist Bluma Zeigarnik discovered that people remember unfinished tasks 90% better than completed ones. The brain keeps "open loops" running in the background, nagging for closure.

This is why you can't stop watching a series after a cliffhanger. It's why puzzles you almost solved haunt you in the shower. Every great adventure game weaponizes this.

Principle â€” Open Loops Are Addiction

Always have at least 2-3 unresolved questions running simultaneously:

  • A character tease â€” Why did Chrysostomos look sad when I mentioned my father? (background loop, resolved later)

When one loop closes, immediately open a new one. The player should never have zero open questions. Zero open questions = "I guess I'm done" = they close the game.

8.5 Mirror Neurons and Empathy +

In the 1990s, neuroscientists discovered mirror neurons â€” brain cells that fire both when you do something AND when you watch someone else do the same thing. When you see someone smile, your mirror neurons fire as if you were smiling. This is the biological basis of empathy.
